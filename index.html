What is Cryptography?

  • Greek: kryptos (hidden), graphein (writing)
  • Study of secret communication in the presence of third parties
    • The message is to be known only by those intended to know about it.
  • Encryption is the process of encoding or "translating" information in a way that only the intended recipient can read it. Decryption is the opposite process.
  • A Cipher is a an algorithm for encrypting and decrypting.
  • No cipher is completely secure (except one!): an attacker's best friend is time.

Ciphers

  • Every cipher of is composed of two parts:
    1. Encryption/Decryption Algorithm: the step-by-step procedure by which a message is encoded/decoded.
    2. Key Space: The set of all possible keys.
  • The key is what makes each encryption unique: encrypting the same message with different keys will give very different results.
  • Generally, the algorith is public knowledge (Kerchoff's Principle). What must be kept secret is the key.
  • There are two main types of ciphers:
    1. Symmetric-key Ciphers: the same key is used for both encryption and decryption.
    2. Asymmetric-key Ciphers: different keys are used.

Encryption: Shift Cipher

  • Works by shifting each letter in a message by a specific amount. This shift number is the key.
  • Weaknesses
    • Easily brute-forced
    • Vulnerable to Frequency Analysis
  • The most famous shift cipher is the Caesar cipher, where each each word is shifted by 3. E.g.

    • Shift of +3 Shift of -3
    "A" becomes "D" "A" becomes "X"
    "B" becomes "E" "B" becomes "Y"
    "C" becomes "F" "C" becomes "Z"

    Encryption: Polyalphabetic Cipher

    • The individuals sharing secret information agree on a secret word.
    • The character position of each individual character in that secret word is used to shift the message you are hiding.
    • Example:
      • Secret word: Snake (Shift of 19,14,1,11,5)
      • Secret message: Imperial March
      • Shift every letter in the message "Imperial March" in the sequence of 19-14-1-11-5
        • Shift I by 19       Shift M by 14       Shift P by 1       Shift E by 11       Shift R by 5       Shift I by 19
        • Notice how we shift by 19 again on the second I of "Imperial".
        • You keep reusing the same sequence of shifts until you reach the end of your message.
    • Weakness: Frequency Analysis

    Encryption: The One-time pad

    • The individuals sharing secret information agree on a secret shift, random sequence.
    • Each letter on the message to be encrypted is shifted based on the corresponding number in the sequence.
    • The shift sequence has to be as long as the message you want to encrypt.
    • Shift every letter in the message "Imperial March" randomly
      • Shift I by 1       Shift M by 5       Shift P by 4       Shift E by 6       Shift R by 7      
      • You continue to shift by a random set of numbers that you and the recipient agree on.
    • Claude Shannon proved that the One-time pad has perfect secrecy. It is impossible to break!
    • The problem? The One-time pad is too impractical to be put into practice.

    Attacking Ciphers

    • A cipher is said to be broken if a third party can recover the message or secret key from the encrypted transmission.
    • There are three general ways of attacking ciphers:
      1. Brute-force attacks: try every possible key until the message is succesfully decrypted.
      2. Theoretical attacks: the mathematical theory which the cipher is based on is flawed or not strong enough.
      3. Implementation attacks: the way the cipher is programmed and coded is flawed and and can be exploited. E.g. timming attacks
